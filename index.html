Internet Infrastructure at Home

Examples of physical internet found in the average Wellington household

Modem

Short for modulator-demodulator. A modem is a device that enables a computer to transmit data over, for example, telephone or cable lines. Computer information is stored digitally, whereas information transmitted over telephone lines is transmitted in the form of analog waves. A modem converts between these two forms.

Router

A wireless router is a device in a wireless local area network (WLAN) that determines the next network point to which a packet should be forwarded toward its destination. A wireless router usually connects to a modem, and creates a WiFi signal in order to forward the internet information converted with the modem to WiFi enabled devices.

Ethernet

An Ethernet cable is one of the most common forms of network cable used on wired networks. Ethernet cables connect devices within a local area network, like PCs, routers, and switches. It is commonly used as the bridging cable between a modem and wireless router, and can also be used to connect a computer directly to a router for faster access to the internet.

Internet Infrastructure on the Street

Examples of the internet in physical form that you may notice around Wellington streets

Chorus manhole

Spotted on Cuba Street. Chorus is a New Zealand telecommunications infrastructure company, they are responsible for 70% of the country's Ultra Fast Broadband (UFB) fibre network. These manholes are designed so that authorised individuals can access the underground internet cables for the purpose of maintenance or inspection.

Cell tower

These cell towers house electronic communications equipment along with an antenna to support cellular communication in a network. They are scattered throughout Wellington and emit a signal that is picked up by a cellular devices, allowing the population to access the internet from their mobile phones.

Bus timetable

These e-bus timetables are a common sight throughout Wellington city. They track buses in real time with GPS software in order to more accurately estimate each bus' arrival to that stop. These communicate with the bus network through underground cables that connect to the grid.

Security camera

These are commonly found in car park buildings and other publicly accessible private properties around the city. Presumably they are connected and monitored via WiFi for real time access to footage.
